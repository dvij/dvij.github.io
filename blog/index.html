Finding the Best Equilibrium in Potential Games

Potential games arise naturally in routing, economics, and evolutionary biology. Finding the welfare-maximizing Nash equilibrium is NP-hard in general — but a new reparameterization argument identifies a large, natural tractable class, certified by an SDP with just n+1 constraints.

Motivating Problems

The problem at the heart of this post is: given a quadratic potential game on the simplex, find the Nash equilibrium that maximizes aggregate welfare. This is NP-hard in general (Conitzer & Sandholm, 2003). But before the main result, it is worth understanding where this problem genuinely arises, what the tractable class means in each setting, and why finding the global optimum matters.

The model is this: a population mixes over n pure strategies, with state x \in \Delta_n. The payoff to strategy i at state x is \pi_i(x) = (Ax + q)_i, where A_{ij} captures how the frequency of strategy j affects the payoff to i, and q_i is the intrinsic payoff — how valuable strategy i is independent of the current mix. The potential is V(x) = x^\top A x + q^\top x, and Nash equilibria are exactly its KKT points on \Delta_n.

Evolutionary ecology: species coexistence

Consider n species competing for overlapping sets of resources. The state x \in \Delta_n is the relative abundance distribution. The payoff (per-capita growth rate) to species i at state x is:

\pi_i(x) = q_i - \sum_j A_{ij} x_j

where q_i > 0 is species i’s intrinsic growth rate (how fast it grows in an empty environment), and A_{ij} \geq 0 is the competitive effect of species j on species i (resource overlap). This is the classical Lotka-Volterra competition model in population-game form. The potential is V(x) = q^\top x - x^\top A x / 2 (equivalently, maximize -V for minimizing competition losses).

Stable coexistence equilibria — states where multiple species persist — are local maxima of V. The ecologically optimal equilibrium maximizes total productivity \sum_i x_i q_i net of competition losses. When species are ecologically differentiated — distinct niches, so A is nearly diagonal — the potential is concave and there is a unique coexistence equilibrium. As niche overlap grows, A develops large off-diagonal entries, the potential becomes indefinite, and multiple stable equilibria appear: different subsets of species can dominate depending on history.

What the tractable class means here: the SDP is feasible when intrinsic growth rates q_i are large relative to the indefiniteness induced by competitive overlaps. In ecological terms: when species have strong intrinsic fitness advantages that make their persistence robust, the global welfare-maximizing coexistence state can be found in polynomial time. The excluded regime — q = 0, pure competition for fixed resources — is exactly Motzkin-Straus (equivalent to max clique), and is always intractable.

Population games: technology adoption with network effects

A population of agents adopts one of n technologies. The state x \in \Delta_n is the adoption distribution. The payoff to technology i has two parts: an intrinsic quality q_i > 0 (how useful the technology is standalone), and network effects captured by A_{ij}: positive A_{ij} means technologies i and j are complementary (each becomes more valuable when the other is widely used), negative means substitutes (competing for the same use case).

Nash equilibria are stable adoption distributions — states where no agent wants to unilaterally switch. Multiple equilibria exist when network effects are strong and mixed: a technology can be locked in at high adoption even if it is not the welfare-maximizing choice, and a welfare-superior technology can fail to gain traction.

The welfare-maximizing equilibrium is the adoption distribution that maximizes aggregate utility V(x) = x^\top A x + q^\top x. Finding it matters for a regulator or platform designer choosing which technology to promote, which standards to subsidize, or which ecosystem to build around.

What the tractable class means here: the SDP is feasible when intrinsic quality differences q are large relative to the cross-technology interaction structure. Concretely: if technologies have meaningfully differentiated standalone value — not just pure network-effect plays where quality is zero without adoption — then the welfare-maximizing adoption equilibrium can be computed exactly. The hard case is pure coordination games (q = 0), where the equilibrium is entirely determined by self-reinforcing adoption dynamics.

The common structure

Both examples share the same pattern. The interaction matrix A can be indefinite — combining complementarity (positive entries) and competition (negative entries) — creating multiple local optima on \Delta_n. The linear term q > 0 breaks symmetry by assigning standalone value to each strategy. The tractable class is precisely the regime where q is strong enough relative to A’s indefiniteness that a single reparameterization can globally concavify V. Within this class, the global welfare maximum is computationally accessible; outside it, the problem is generically hard.

The Mathematical Structure

Both settings above have the same mathematical core. Whether we are maximizing ecological productivity under competitive constraints, or finding the welfare-optimal technology adoption equilibrium, the problem reduces to optimizing a quadratic function over the simplex. With n strategies and state x \in \Delta_n = \{x \geq 0 : \mathbf{1}^\top x = 1\}, the potential is:

V(x) = x^\top A x + q^\top x

Here A_{ij} captures the interaction between strategies i and j — how much the fitness of strategy i depends on the frequency of j — and q_i is the intrinsic payoff to strategy i, independent of the population state. The gradient \nabla_i V(x) = 2(Ax)_i + q_i is the marginal payoff to strategy i at state x.

Nash equilibria of this game are exactly the KKT points of \max_{x \in \Delta_n} V(x): states where all active strategies have equal (and maximal) payoff, and no inactive strategy has higher payoff. The welfare-maximizing Nash equilibrium is simply the global maximum of V over \Delta_n.

So the problem reduces to:

\max_{x \in \Delta_n}\; x^\top A x + q^\top x \tag{P}

This is a quadratic program on the simplex. When A \preceq 0, the potential V is concave, has a unique global maximum, and (P) is easy — any local solver finds it. The hard and interesting case is when A is indefinite: some eigenvalues positive, some negative. This is generic in practice, since strategic interactions have no reason to be sign-definite.

Why This Is Hard in General

  1. with indefinite A is a nonconvex quadratic program, and nonconvex QPs are NP-hard. But the hardness here is not just a worst-case curiosity — it reflects a real structural difficulty.

Multiple local optima. An indefinite V can have exponentially many local maxima on \Delta_n, each a stable Nash equilibrium. The replicator dynamics and most learning algorithms converge to some local maximum, but which one depends on initialization. The global maximum can be arbitrarily better than a typical local one.

No useful SDP relaxation in general. The standard approach to nonconvex QPs is to relax to a semidefinite program. For this problem the SDP relaxation is:

\max\; \langle \hat{A}, \hat{X} \rangle \quad\text{s.t.}\quad \hat{X} \succeq 0,\; \hat{X}_{00} = 1,\; \text{simplex constraints on } \hat{X}

This is solvable in polynomial time, but the relaxation is generally not tight for indefinite A — the SDP optimum can exceed the true optimum, and rank-1 recovery fails.

Inapproximability. Conitzer and Sandholm (2003) showed that finding a Nash equilibrium maximizing social welfare is NP-hard, and moreover inapproximable in polynomial time: no algorithm can guarantee being within any constant factor of optimal welfare, unless P = NP. This rules out not just exact algorithms but any efficient approximation scheme for the general case.

The picture is bleak. And yet — for a large, natural subclass of games, the problem turns out to be exactly solvable in polynomial time.

A Tractable Class via Reparameterization

The key idea

Rather than relaxing (P) to an SDP, we ask a different question: can we find a change of variables x_i = s_i(y_i) — applied componentwise, one coordinate at a time — that makes the objective concave in y?

If such a reparameterization exists, the problem

\max_{y \in \mathcal{S}'}\; V(s(y))

is a concave maximization over a convex set, solvable exactly by any first-order method. The global maximum of V over \Delta_n is then recovered as x^* = s(y^*).

When does it work?

For a componentwise map x_i = s_i(y_i) with s_i smooth and s_i' > 0, the Hessian of V(s(y)) is:

H(y) = \text{diag}(s') \cdot A \cdot \text{diag}(s') + \text{diag}(\nabla V(x) \circ s'')

For concavity we need H(y) \preceq 0, or equivalently (dividing through by \text{diag}(s')^2):

A + \text{diag}(\kappa \circ \nabla V(x)) \preceq 0 \qquad \forall\, x \in \Delta_n

where \kappa_i = s_i'' / (s_i')^2 is the curvature of the reparameterization. For constant curvature \kappa_i = c_i, this becomes a condition on the matrix

M(x) := A + \text{diag}(c \circ \nabla V(x))

which is affine in x. The condition M(x) \preceq 0 for all x \in \Delta_n is therefore a condition on the minimum eigenvalue of an affine matrix pencil — and minimum eigenvalue is concave. A concave function on a simplex attains its minimum at a vertex.

The certification SDP

The simplex \Delta_n has exactly n+1 vertices: v_0 = 0 and v_i = e_i for i = 1, \ldots, n. By the argument above, checking M(x) \preceq 0 everywhere reduces to checking it at these n+1 points. This gives a certification SDP:

\text{find}\; c \geq 0 \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; A + \text{diag}(c \circ \nabla V(v_k)) \preceq 0, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n \tag{SDP}

with n+1 LMI constraints of size n \times n. If (SDP) is feasible, the reparameterization x_i = -\frac{1}{c_i}\log(1 - c_i y_i) (or x_i = y_i when c_i = 0) concavifies V globally over \Delta_n. We require c \geq 0 to ensure the maps s_i are convex, which keeps the reparameterized domain convex.

The full pipeline is:

  1. Certify: solve (SDP). Polynomial time, n+1 LMIs.
  2. Solve: maximize the reparameterized (now concave) V(s(y)) over the convex y-domain with any gradient method.
  3. Recover: x^* = s(y^*) is the global welfare-maximizing Nash equilibrium.

Why the simplex is special

For a box domain [0,1]^n, the same argument requires checking 2^n vertices — exponential, and useless for large n. The simplex has only n+1 vertices. This is not a coincidence: the simplex is the natural domain for mixed strategies precisely because it has this lean vertex structure. The geometry of the strategy space is doing real work here.

What Games Are in the Tractable Class?

The feasibility of (SDP) depends on both A and q. At vertex v_0 = 0, the condition becomes A + \text{diag}(c \circ q) \preceq 0. For c \geq 0, this requires q \geq 0 (intrinsic payoffs must be nonnegative) and c_i q_i large enough to dominate the positive eigenvalues of A. At vertex v_i = e_i, the condition involves \nabla V(e_i) = 2Ae_i + q, which mixes the interaction and intrinsic terms.

Intuitively, the SDP is feasible when the intrinsic payoffs q are strong relative to the indefiniteness of A. This is the regime where each strategy has a strong standalone reason to be played, and the strategic interactions are secondary perturbations. In the ecological example: species have strong intrinsic growth rates that make their persistence robust to competitive pressure. In the technology adoption example: technologies have meaningfully differentiated standalone quality, not just pure network-effect value.

This gives the tractable class a clean interpretation: games where identity dominates interaction. Strategies matter for what they are, not just for what they do to each other. The certifiability condition quantifies precisely how strong this dominance needs to be.

What’s excluded: games where strategic interactions dominate (A strongly indefinite, q small or zero). In particular, homogeneous games (q = 0) are generically outside the tractable class. The Motzkin-Straus problem — finding the maximum clique via \max x^\top A_G x on the simplex with q=0 — is NP-hard, and is always outside the certifiable class for nontrivial graphs. In ecological terms this is pure competition with no intrinsic fitness differences: any outcome is as good as any other in isolation, and history entirely determines which species dominate. This is a sanity check: the hardness of max clique is not accidentally circumvented.

The tractable class is convex in (A, q) space — it’s the projection of an SDP feasible set. This means it can be tested efficiently, and it degrades gracefully: as \|q\| grows relative to \|A\|, more and more games become certifiable.

Numerical evidence

On 500 random indefinite quadratic potential games (n = 4, q \sim 3\mathcal{N}(0,I)):

Count Fraction
Certifiably tractable 411 82%
— with sign-changing gradient (nontrivial) 220 54% of tractable
— with interior global optimum 29 13% of nontrivial
Not certifiable 89 18%

The 29 instances with interior optima are the most significant. The global welfare-maximizing equilibrium is a fully mixed strategy — not achievable by vertex enumeration — and the reparameterization pipeline finds it exactly. All instances were validated against brute-force global search.

The dynamical corollary

In potential games, the replicator dynamics converge to Nash equilibria — but generically to local maxima of V, not the global one. In the certifiable class, after reparameterization, V has a unique global maximum on \Delta_n. This means:

Note

Corollary. For potential games in the certifiable class, the replicator dynamics select the welfare-maximizing Nash equilibrium from almost all initial conditions — and this equilibrium can be computed in polynomial time.

Computational tractability and dynamical stability coincide. Outside the certifiable class, these can come apart: the dynamics may select a suboptimal equilibrium, and finding the optimal one is hard.

Open Questions

Characterizing the boundary. The certifiable class is SDP-defined but has no simple closed-form description. Is there a spectral condition on (A, q) — e.g., certifiable if and only if \lambda_{\max}(A) \leq f(\|q\|_\infty, n) for some explicit f — that captures the phase transition?

Beyond constant curvature. Allowing \kappa_i(x_i) = a_i + b_i x_i (affine curvature) makes M(x) quadratic in x, breaking the vertex check. Matrix SOS techniques could certify this on the simplex at higher cost. Does the larger family of reparameterizations capture significantly more games?

Connection to known tractable classes. The classical results on tractable QCQPs — the S-lemma for one quadratic constraint, Burer & Ye’s “at most one negative eigenvalue” condition — work via Lagrange multipliers that convexify the dual. The reparameterization works on the primal. Do the two tractable classes overlap, or is one a strict generalization of the other?

Branch-and-bound on sub-simplices. For games outside the certifiable class, the simplex can be subdivided. On sub-simplices, the gradient \nabla V has a more consistent sign, and (SDP) becomes easier. Can systematic subdivision give a practical exact algorithm even for hard instances?
