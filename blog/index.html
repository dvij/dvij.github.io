Tractable QCQPs: From the S-Lemma to Reparameterization on the Simplex

optimization
theory
semidefinite-programming
A tour through sixty years of hidden convexity in quadratically constrained programs — from Yakubovich’s S-lemma and the trust-region subproblem, through Burer & Ye’s unifying spectral condition, to a new reparameterization technique that certifies global tractability for nonconvex QPs on the simplex with only n+1 LMI constraints.
Author

Dj

Published

March 6, 2026

Quadratically constrained quadratic programs (QCQPs) are NP-hard in general. Yet a surprising swath of instances that arise in practice admit exact polynomial-time solutions, often through semidefinite programming (SDP) relaxation. The story of why is one of the more beautiful threads in modern optimization theory — starting with a 1971 theorem about feedback control, running through the trust-region subproblem and Max-Cut, and arriving at some recent unifying results.

This post traces that story and then describes a new angle: a reparameterization-based approach that certifies tractability for nonconvex QPs on the simplex via an SDP with only n+1 constraints, compared to 2^n for the analogous box result.

Setup

A QCQP takes the form:

\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \; x^\top A_0 x + 2b_0^\top x + c_0 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad x^\top A_i x + 2b_i^\top x + c_i \leq 0, \quad i = 1, \ldots, m

with A_i \in \mathbb{S}^n symmetric but not necessarily positive semidefinite. The canonical SDP relaxation lifts to X = xx^\top, relaxes the rank-1 constraint to X \succeq 0, and solves the resulting semidefinite program. The relaxation is exact when the SDP and QCQP optima coincide and a rank-1 optimizer can be recovered.

The question is: when does this happen?

Part I: The Classical Results

The S-Lemma (1971)

The oldest and most fundamental result is Yakubovich’s S-lemma.

Note

Theorem (S-Lemma, Yakubovich 1971). Let f(x) = x^\top A_0 x + 2b_0^\top x + c_0 and g(x) = x^\top A_1 x + 2b_1^\top x + c_1. Assume \exists\,\bar{x} with g(\bar{x}) < 0 (Slater condition). Then: f(x) \geq 0 \;\forall x: g(x) \leq 0 \quad\iff\quad \exists\,\lambda \geq 0:\; f(x) - \lambda g(x) \geq 0 \;\forall x.

In control theory this says: if a quadratic Lyapunov function is positive on all trajectories satisfying a quadratic constraint, there is a single multiplier that certifies it globally.

The consequence for optimization is immediate: for m=1, the SDP relaxation is always exact under Slater’s condition. The dual multiplier \lambda provides a zero duality gap certificate, and rank-1 recovery follows from the Barvinok-Pataki rank bound (r(r+1)/2 \leq m+1 forces r=1 when m=1).

The S-lemma fails for m=2 in general — counterexamples exist already in \mathbb{R}^2.

The Trust-Region Subproblem. A single equality constraint \|x\|^2 = 1 is covered by a generalized S-lemma (the multiplier \lambda is unrestricted in sign), making the trust-region subproblem

\min_{x}\; x^\top A_0 x + 2b_0^\top x \quad\text{s.t.}\quad \|x\|^2 \leq 1

exactly solvable in near-linear time via the secular equation. This is the inner loop of virtually every trust-region optimization method.

The CDT Problem. Ye & Zhang (2003) extended exactness to two ball constraints (the “CDT problem”) under mild nondegeneracy, establishing m=2 ball-constrained QCQPs as tractable.

The complex domain. Over \mathbb{C}^n, the situation is uniformly better: the SDP relaxation is exact for m \leq 2 with no additional assumptions (Huang & Zhang 2007), and for m \leq 3 rank-1 constraints (So 2010). The Barvinok-Pataki bound is more generous over \mathbb{C} because complex matrices have more degrees of freedom.

Goemans-Williamson and Boolean QCQPs (1995)

A qualitatively different class: Boolean QCQPs with x_i \in \{-1,+1\} (equivalently x_i^2 = 1). The Max-Cut problem is the canonical instance. Goemans and Williamson showed the SDP relaxation gives a 0.878-approximation via randomized hyperplane rounding — a landmark result that launched the field of approximation algorithms via SDP. For bipartite graphs the relaxation is exact.

Part II: Unifying Results

Joint Sign Definiteness

The deepest unifying condition in the real case is joint sign definiteness: the QCQP is jointly definite if there exist \lambda_i \geq 0 such that

A_0 + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i A_i \succeq 0.

In words: the dual multipliers can convexify the Lagrangian. Burer (2012) showed this implies exact SDP relaxation. But the real sharpening came with Burer & Ye (2020):

Important

Theorem (Burer & Ye 2020). If the Lagrangian Hessian A_0 + \sum_i \lambda_i^* A_i at the optimal dual \lambda^* has at most one negative eigenvalue, the SDP relaxation is exact.

This is the tightest known unifying condition for real QCQPs. It subsumes convex QCQPs, the S-lemma / TRS, joint definiteness, and the simultaneously-diagonalizable case of Huang & Zhang (2007) as special cases. The bound is tight: problems where the Lagrangian has \geq 2 negative eigenvalues can be NP-hard.

Chordal Sparsity as a Decomposition Engine

A third structural condition, orthogonal to the above, is chordal sparsity. Associate a graph G to the QCQP where edges indicate nonzero interactions among variables. If G is chordal, its maximal cliques form a perfect elimination ordering and the global SDP decomposes into clique-local SDPs:

X \succeq 0 \iff X_{C_\ell} \succeq 0 \;\forall\,\ell \quad \text{(plus consistency)}

(Kim & Kojima 2003; Vandenberghe & Andersen 2015). If each clique subproblem is tractable via the S-lemma or joint definiteness, the whole problem is tractable in time polynomial in n for fixed treewidth.

All of the above approach tractability from the dual side: find Lagrange multipliers that convexify the problem, or decompose via sparsity. The next section describes a complementary primal approach.

Part III: Reparameterization on the Simplex

The Setup

Consider a nonconvex QP on the simplex:

\min_{x \in \mathcal{S}}\; \phi(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}x^\top Qx + q^\top x, \qquad \mathcal{S} = \{x \geq 0 : \mathbf{1}^\top x \leq R\}

where Q \in \mathbb{S}^n is indefinite. We ask: can we find a componentwise reparameterization x_i = s_i(y_i) that makes the problem convex in y?

The Reparameterization Condition

For x = s(y) with s_i smooth and s_i' > 0, the Hessian of \phi(s(y)) with respect to y is:

H_\phi(y) = \text{diag}(s'(y))\cdot Q\cdot\text{diag}(s'(y)) + \text{diag}(g(x) \circ s''(y))

where g(x) = Qx + q is the gradient of \phi at x = s(y). For convexity we need H_\phi(y) \succeq 0. Dividing through by \text{diag}(s'(y)), this is equivalent to:

M(x) := Q + \text{diag}(\kappa \circ g(x)) \succeq 0

where \kappa_i = s_i''(y_i)/s_i'(y_i)^2 is the reparameterization curvature. For constant \kappa_i = c_i, the ODE s''/s'^2 = c has explicit solution s(y) = -\frac{1}{c}\log(1 - cy) for c > 0 (recovering s(y) = y as c \to 0).

The key structural fact: M(x) = Q + \text{diag}(c \circ g(x)) is affine in x. Therefore x \mapsto \lambda_{\min}(M(x)) is concave on any convex domain.

Efficient Certification via Vertex Checking

Note

Proposition (Vertex Exactness). Since x \mapsto \lambda_{\min}(M(x)) is concave and \mathcal{S} is a simplex with n+1 vertices v_0 = 0,\; v_i = R\,e_i, the condition M(x) \succeq 0 for all x \in \mathcal{S} is equivalent to: Q + \text{diag}(c \circ g(v_k)) \succeq 0, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n.

This gives a certification SDP with n+1 LMI constraints of size n \times n:

\text{find } c \geq 0 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad Q + \text{diag}(c \circ g(v_k)) \succeq 0, \quad k = 0, \ldots, n \tag{SDP}

If feasible, the reparameterization x_i = s_i(y_i) = -\frac{1}{c_i}\log(1-c_i y_i) (or y_i if c_i=0) convexifies \phi globally over \mathcal{S}.

The contrast with box domains is stark: a box [0,R]^n has 2^n vertices, making the analogous certification exponential in n. The simplex has n+1 — linear in n.

Note also that c \geq 0 is required for the reparameterization s_i to be convex (ensuring the y-domain \{\sum_i s_i(y_i) \leq R\} is convex). This restricts the correction to the positive gradient regime — instances where g_i(x) > 0 over most of \mathcal{S} — which is the practically relevant case in, e.g., portfolio optimization with positive expected returns.

The Certify-and-Solve Pipeline

Once (SDP) is feasible with solution c^* \geq 0:

  1. Certify: the map x_i = s_i(y_i) = -\frac{1}{c_i^*}\log(1 - c_i^* y_i) convexifies \phi over \mathcal{S}.

  2. Solve: minimize the reparameterized (now convex) objective over the convex y-domain using any first-order method. The gradient is \nabla_{y_i}\phi = s_i'(y_i) \cdot g_i(s(y)) = e^{c_i^* y_i} \cdot g_i(s(y)), computable in O(n^2) per step.

  3. Recover: x^* = s(y^*) is the global optimum of the original problem.

Numerical Validation

On 500 random indefinite instances (n=4, Q indefinite, q \sim 3\mathcal{N}(0,I), simplex radius R=1):

Outcome Count Fraction
SDP feasible (convexifiable) 411 82%
— with sign-changing g_i (nontrivial) 220 54% of feasible
— with interior global optimum 29 13% of nontrivial
SDP infeasible 89 18%

The 29 instances with interior optima are the most interesting: the global optimum is not at any vertex of \mathcal{S}, so naive vertex enumeration would fail. The reparameterization finds them exactly. All feasible instances were validated by comparison with a brute-force global search (200,000 random samples + vertex check), with optima matching to within 10^{-2}.

Robust Certification

Since (SDP) is affine in Q, certifying convexifiability for all Q in a polytopic uncertainty set \mathcal{Q} = \text{conv}\{Q_1, \ldots, Q_p\} requires only p \times (n+1) LMI constraints — still polynomial. A single SDP certifies the entire family, which is useful in portfolio optimization where the covariance matrix is estimated with uncertainty.

Where This Fits in the Landscape

The reparameterization approach is complementary to the Lagrangian duality results of Part II:

  • Vs. the S-lemma: the S-lemma handles one quadratic constraint; reparameterization handles an indefinite quadratic objective on a simplex domain. Different structural conditions.

  • Vs. joint definiteness: both ultimately produce a convex problem, but via dual (Lagrange multipliers) vs. primal (change of variables) paths. It is an open question whether the two tractable classes coincide or one properly extends the other.

  • Vs. chordal decomposition: chordal methods exploit sparsity of Q; reparameterization exploits gradient sign structure and domain geometry. The two can in principle be combined.

A fundamental limitation: The componentwise structure — each x_i depends only on y_i — is what gives the diagonal correction and enables the LMI formulation. Non-componentwise maps could potentially reach more instances but would destroy the LMI structure, making certification intractable. This tradeoff between expressiveness and certifiability is a core tension.

Open Questions

A few directions that seem worth pursuing:

Affine \kappa. Allowing \kappa_i(x_i) = a_i + b_i x_i makes M(x) quadratic in x, breaking the vertex check. Matrix SOS techniques on the simplex could certify this at higher cost — is the tradeoff worthwhile?

Intrinsic characterization. Is there a simple spectral condition on (Q, q) that characterizes the boundary of the certifiable class without solving an SDP? Something like: convexifiable when \min_i q_i > h(\lambda_{\min}(Q), n, R) for some explicit function h?

Integration with branch-and-bound. On sub-simplices created by branching, fewer gradient components change sign, so (SDP) becomes easier. Can reparameterization provide exact (not relaxed) bounds within B&B?

Connection to hidden convexity. Does the reparameterization-tractable class intersect with or properly extend the classical results? A precise comparison would clarify whether this is genuinely new or a reparameterization of a known tractable class.
