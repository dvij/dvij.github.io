The problem: which equilibrium is best?

A potential game has a scalar function V(x) — the potential — whose stationary points on the simplex \Delta_n are exactly the Nash equilibria. Potential games arise naturally in several contexts, including modeling traffic congestion and evolutionary game theory. In congestion games, V captures route quality: Nash equilibria are traffic patterns where no commuter wants to switch routes, and the socially optimal equilibrium is the one that maximizes V.

When V is concave, maximization is easy — gradient ascent finds the unique global maximum. When V is convex, the maximum is always at a vertex and we just check n+1 points. But generically V is indefinite: neither convex nor concave. Multiple equilibria coexist with very different welfare, and finding the best one requires searching the entire simplex — exponentially many faces.

The question: find the Nash equilibrium that maximizes welfare — the global maximum of V on \Delta_n.

This is NP-hard. Conitzer & Sandholm (2003) showed it is even inapproximable: no polynomial-time algorithm can guarantee any constant fraction of optimal welfare, unless P = NP.

This post describes a way around the hardness for a large natural subclass. A componentwise change of variables x_i = s_i(y_i) can make V globally concave in y, turning the indefinite problem into a concave maximization. Whether such a change exists is checkable by a small SDP with just n+1 constraints. When it does, the welfare-maximizing equilibrium is computable in polynomial time.

A worked example: morning commute

Setup

A unit mass of commuters splits among n = 3 routes.

Route Description Free-flow time Key feature
1 — Highway Through city center 25 min Fast but bottleneck-prone
2 — Bypass Six-lane ring road 32 min High capacity, long
3 — Local Two-lane suburban road 28 min Moderate; merges with both others

The commuter fractions x = (x_1, x_2, x_3) \in \Delta_3 determine welfare via the potential:

V(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\, x^\top Q\, x + q^\top x

with:

Q = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0.5 \\ -1 & 0.5 & 1 \\ 0.5 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad q = \begin{pmatrix} 4.5 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.

The vector q captures free-flow quality — each route’s payoff before any congestion. The matrix Q captures pairwise congestion interactions:

  • Diagonal entries (Q_{11} = 2 > Q_{33} = 1 > Q_{22} = 0.5): self-interaction strength. The highway interacts most strongly with itself (narrow bottleneck), the bypass least (wide road).
  • Q_{12} = -1 (negative = complementary): splitting traffic between the highway and bypass relieves congestion — separate corridors, no shared bottleneck.
  • Q_{13} = 0.5, Q_{23} = 1 (positive = competing for capacity): the local road shares a merge point with both other routes, creating cross-congestion.

Why the problem is hard

Q is indefinite: eigenvalues \approx +2.73, +0.54, -0.77. Since V is neither convex nor concave, we cannot apply standard optimization — neither gradient ascent (no concavity guarantee) nor vertex enumeration (no convexity guarantee) solves the problem. Multiple Nash equilibria coexist, and finding the welfare-maximizing one means globally optimizing an indefinite QP on \Delta_3.

For n = 3 we could brute-force it (enumerate KKT points on all faces), but the NP-hardness means no general-purpose method works for large n.

The reparameterization idea

We ask: is there a componentwise change of variables x_i = s_i(y_i) that makes V concave in y?

If yes: \max_y V(s(y)) is a concave maximization — solvable by gradient ascent. The solution maps back to x^* = s(y^*), the welfare-maximizing equilibrium.

Reducing to a finite check

For maps s_i with constant curvature \kappa_i = s_i''/(s_i')^2, the Hessian of V(s(y)) factors as S' \cdot M(x) \cdot S', where S' = \text{diag}(s_1', \ldots, s_n') is positive and:

M(x) = Q + \text{diag}\!\big(\kappa \circ \nabla V(x)\big)

with \nabla V(x) = Qx + q. Since S' is positive, V(s(y)) is concave if and only if M(x) \preceq 0 for all x \in \Delta_n: the matrix M must be negative semidefinite everywhere on the simplex.

Two key facts make this checkable:

  1. M(x) is affine in x (because \nabla V is), hence matrix-concave.
  2. A matrix-concave function on a polytope achieves its maximum eigenvalue at a vertex.

The simplex \Delta_n has n + 1 vertices. So checking M(x) \preceq 0 everywhere reduces to checking it at n+1 points:

\text{find}\; \kappa \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; M(v_k) \preceq 0, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n \tag{SDP}

This is a semidefinite program with n+1 matrix constraints of size n \times n — polynomial time.

Note

Why the simplex matters. On a box [0,1]^n, the same argument requires 2^n vertex checks. The simplex has only n+1. The lean geometry of the strategy space makes the method efficient.

Matrix-concavity is what matters. The vertex trick does not require M(x) to be affine — only matrix-concave. Since M(x) modifies only the diagonal of Q, matrix-concavity reduces to requiring each diagonal entry M_{ii}(x) to be concave in x. With constant curvature \kappa_i these entries are affine (a special case), but richer reparameterizations where \kappa_i depends on x_i can produce concave diagonals while preserving the finite check.

Certifying the routing game

Eliminate x_3 = 1 - x_1 - x_2 to reduce to n = 2 on the standard simplex \{y \geq 0, \mathbf{1}^\top y \leq 1\}:

\tilde{Q} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1.5 \\ -1.5 & -0.5 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \tilde{q} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}.

This reduced matrix is indefinite (\det \tilde{Q} = -3.25). The nonconvexity does not go away.

The check

Three vertices, three gradient vectors g(v) = \tilde{Q}v + \tilde{q}, three certification matrices M(v) = \tilde{Q} + \kappa \cdot \text{diag}(g(v)).

With \kappa = -1.70 (concave reparameterization maps):

Vertex All commuters on… g(v) \det M(v) \text{tr}\,M(v)
(1,0) Highway (5.0,\; 1.5) +17.58 -9.55
(0,1) Bypass (1.5,\; 2.5) +0.36 -5.30
(0,0) Local road (3.0,\; 3.0) +15.11 -8.70

At every vertex, M(v) has positive determinant and negative trace — both eigenvalues are negative, so M(v) \prec 0. By concavity of \lambda_{\min} (equivalently, convexity of \lambda_{\max}), M(x) \preceq 0 holds everywhere on the simplex.

The game is in the tractable class. After reparameterization, V is globally concave.

The tightest constraint

Vertex (0,1) — everyone on the bypass — gives the smallest determinant (0.36). There, the highway gradient g_1 = 1.5 provides the weakest diagonal correction: the highway isn’t very attractive when nobody uses it (the complementarity benefit Q_{12} = -1 hasn’t kicked in). This produces the tightest NSD margin.

Had q been less differentiated — say (2, 2, 1) instead of (4.5, 4, 1) — the gradients would shrink and certification would fail. Feasibility requires that routes differ enough in intrinsic quality for the congestion interactions to be secondary.

The global optimum

With certification in hand, apply the concave reparameterization and run gradient ascent on V(s(y)). Since V is now globally concave, there is a unique local (and therefore global) maximum:

x^* = (1, 0, 0), \qquad V(x^*) = 5.5.

All commuters on the highway — the route with the best free-flow quality (q_1 = 4.5). Its high self-congestion (Q_{11} = 2) is dominated by the intrinsic advantage. Mixing in the bypass might seem tempting — the complementarity Q_{12} = -1 provides congestion relief — but the certificate tells us no mixed split improves on the pure highway. (See verify_routing_game.py for a self-contained script that verifies all numerical claims.)

A non-vertex optimum: platform adoption

The routing example has its optimum at a vertex. To see the method find a mixed optimum, consider a platform adoption game with n = 3 technologies.

Q = \begin{pmatrix} -3 & 3.5 & 0.5 \\ 3.5 & 3.5 & 3 \\ 0.5 & 3 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad q = \begin{pmatrix} 17 \\ 14 \\ 12 \end{pmatrix}.

Here Q_{12} = 3.5 > 0: platforms 1 and 2 are synergistic — adopting both together creates positive network effects (e.g., interoperable standards). Meanwhile Q_{11} = -3 < 0: concentrating entirely on platform 1 has diminishing returns (saturation). The reduced matrix \tilde{Q} = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} -2 & 2 \\ 2 & -0.5 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] is indefinite (\det = -3).

Certification

With \kappa = -0.3, the NSD check passes at all three vertices:

Vertex g(v) \det M(v) \text{tr}\,M(v)
(1,0) (1.5,\; 5.0) +0.90 -4.45
(0,1) (5.5,\; 2.5) +0.56 -4.90
(0,0) (3.5,\; 3.0) +0.27 -4.45

The optimum

Gradient ascent in the reparameterized space converges to the unique global maximum:

x^* \approx (0.46,\; 0.54,\; 0), \qquad V(x^*) = 16.44.

Users split roughly half-and-half between the two synergistic platforms; nobody adopts platform 3. This mixed equilibrium is not at any vertex — it cannot be found by evaluating V at pure strategies. The concavification certificate guarantees this is the unique global optimum.

The bigger picture

The tractable class

The SDP is feasible whenever intrinsic payoffs dominate strategic interactions — q is large relative to Q’s indefiniteness. This defines a tractable class: the set of (Q, q) pairs for which the SDP has a solution.

  • Convex in (Q, q) space — membership is efficiently testable.
  • Grows with \|q\| — more games become certifiable as intrinsic payoffs strengthen.
  • Excludes the provably hard cases — q = 0 gives \max x^\top Q x on \Delta_n, an NP-hard problem (Motzkin–Straus / max clique). These are never certifiable.

The pipeline

  1. Certify (poly time): solve the SDP to find curvature parameters \kappa.
  2. Solve (poly time): maximize the now-concave V(s(y)) by gradient ascent.
  3. Recover: x^* = s(y^*) is the welfare-maximizing equilibrium.

How large is the tractable class?

On 500 random indefinite potential games (n = 4, \|q\|/\|Q\| \approx 3):

Count %
Certifiably tractable 411 82%
Not certifiable 89 18%

Of the 411 certifiable games, 29 had interior global optima — fully mixed equilibria where traffic spreads across all routes. The reparameterization found every one exactly, validated against brute-force global search.

Dynamics

In potential games, learning dynamics (replicator, gradient play) converge to some Nash equilibrium — but not necessarily the welfare-maximizing one. In the certifiable class, the reparameterized V is concave with a unique global maximum, so:

Note

Corollary. For games in the certifiable class, gradient-based dynamics converge to the welfare-maximizing equilibrium from almost all initial conditions.

Computational tractability and dynamical selection coincide.

Open questions

A diagonal dominance sufficient condition. The SDP is one way to certify the tractable class. A simpler (but more conservative) sufficient condition comes from diagonal dominance: M(x) \preceq 0 holds whenever each diagonal entry dominates its row, i.e., |\kappa_i| \cdot g_i(x) - Q_{ii} \geq \sum_{j \neq i} |Q_{ij}| for all i and x \in \Delta_n. Since g_i is affine and achieves its minimum at a vertex, this reduces to:

\min_{v \in \text{vert}(\Delta_n)} [Qv + q]_i \;>\; \sum_j |Q_{ij}| \cdot \frac{1}{|\kappa_i|} + Q_{ii}

In words: each strategy’s minimum payoff gradient (over pure-strategy profiles) must exceed the total absolute interaction strength of that strategy. This is the “standalone payoffs dominate interactions” intuition made precise. It is not tight — the SDP exploits correlations between rows — but it gives a closed-form sufficient condition without solving an optimization problem. Can this gap be characterized? Is there a scaled diagonal dominance condition (à la Geršgorin with optimal scaling) that is both closed-form and tight?

Richer reparameterizations via matrix-concavity. Constant-curvature maps make M(x) affine — a special case of matrix-concave. Allowing \kappa_i to depend on x_i (e.g., \kappa_i(x_i) = a_i + b_i x_i) produces diagonal entries that are quadratic in x. If these remain concave, the vertex trick still applies with n+1 checks. Does this broader family — certifiable via the same SDP structure — capture meaningfully more games than constant curvature?

Relation to the S-lemma. The classical S-lemma establishes joint convexity of the numerical range of a pair of quadratic forms. Reparameterization convexifies the primal potential directly. Do these approaches certify the same class of games, or are they fundamentally different?
